# The Concentration of AI Copyright Litigation: A US-Centric Pattern

## Central Argument

While AI copyright issues attract attention globally, the actual litigation and policy controversy is heavily concentrated in the United States. Other major jurisdictions have either resolved the core questions through legislation, administrative guidance, or judicial precedent, or have seen minimal dispute activity. Understanding this distribution is important for comparative research, as it clarifies where live legal questions remain and where frameworks are relatively settled.

## Jurisdiction-by-Jurisdiction Status

### European Union: Largely Settled via Legislation
The Copyright in the Digital Single Market Directive (2019), particularly Articles 3 and 4, directly addresses AI training through text and data mining exceptions:
- **Article 3**: Mandatory TDM exception for research organizations
- **Article 4**: Broader TDM exception for any purpose, subject to rightsholder opt-out

The European Commission's 2023 guidance on AI and copyright confirms that training generative AI models typically falls under Article 4, provided rightsholders have not reserved their rights. European courts have not seen major AI copyright litigation because the legislative framework provides clear rules.

### China: Judicial Precedents Established
As discussed in the companion document on Chinese AI copyright law, Chinese courts have issued multiple rulings (Tencent v. Yingxun, Baidu v. 360, Li v. Liu) that establish a workable framework for AI-generated work protection based on demonstrated human intellectual input. The legal questions are not fully settled for all scenarios, but the basic approach is established.

### United Kingdom: Statutory Framework Since 1988
The UK has had a specific provision for "computer-generated works" in the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 (s.9(3)). While the scope and application to modern generative AI remain debated academically, the basic legal infrastructure exists. The UK IPO's 2023-2024 consultations have explored expanding TDM exceptions rather than litigating infringement questions.

### Japan: Explicit Legislative Permission
Japan amended its Copyright Law in 2018 (effective 2019) to add Article 30-4, which explicitly permits information analysis including AI training, without distinguishing commercial from non-commercial use. The Japan Patent Office's 2023 guidelines confirm this interpretation. Litigation is unnecessary because the law is permissive.

### South Korea: Administrative Resolution
South Korea's 2021 copyright amendments addressed AI training, and the Korean Intellectual Property Office has issued detailed administrative guidelines. The KIPO operates an AI copyright registration system that provides a practical resolution mechanism.

### Other Common Law Jurisdictions
- **Canada**: No reported AI copyright litigation; fair dealing provisions likely cover training
- **Australia**: Copyright Agency is monitoring but no major cases filed; ALRC recommendations favor flexible exceptions
- **India**: Copyright Office issued guidance in 2023 permitting AI-generated work registration under certain conditions
- **Singapore**: 2021 amendments introduced computational analysis exception

### United States: The Active Controversy
The US is distinct in several respects:
1. **No specific AI legislation**: Congress has not passed AI-specific copyright provisions
2. **Common law development**: Issues are being resolved through litigation (Andersen v. Stability AI, Getty v. Stability AI, NYT v. OpenAI, Authors Guild v. OpenAI)
3. **Industry structure**: US-based tech companies (OpenAI, Google, Meta, Anthropic) and major content industries (publishing, music, film) are globally dominant, creating high-stakes disputes
4. **Fair use uncertainty**: The applicability of fair use to AI training is being tested in courts without legislative guidance
5. **Copyright Office policy**: The USCO's Thaler decision and subsequent guidance have created friction by requiring human authorship for registration

## Why the US Is Different

The US concentration of AI copyright disputes reflects structural factors rather than greater legal uncertainty per se:
- **Economic scale**: The US has the largest AI development sector and the largest creative industries; disputes involve the highest dollar values
- **Litigation culture**: The US has a more active plaintiff bar and class-action infrastructure for copyright disputes
- **Regulatory lag**: Other jurisdictions acted legislatively (EU DSM Directive, Japan Article 30-4, Singapore amendments) while the US has relied on courts
- **Fair use flexibility**: The open-ended fair use doctrine generates more litigation because outcomes are less predictable ex ante

## Implications for Comparative Research

For researchers conducting international comparative analysis:
1. The "global AI copyright debate" is substantially a debate about US law, with other jurisdictions having adopted legislative or administrative solutions
2. Comparative studies should distinguish between:
   - **Settled jurisdictions**: Where legislation or precedent provides clear rules (EU, Japan, Singapore)
   - **Evolving jurisdictions**: Where guidance is emerging (UK, China, India)
   - **Litigation-driven jurisdictions**: Where courts are the primary rule-makers (US)
3. Policy recommendations should be jurisdiction-specific; a one-size-fits-all approach ignores these structural differences

## Conclusion

The international landscape of AI copyright is more settled than media coverage suggests. The visible controversies—high-profile lawsuits, Congressional hearings, Copyright Office proceedings—are predominantly US phenomena. Other major jurisdictions have addressed core questions through legislation or administrative guidance, resulting in fewer live disputes. Comparative research should recognize this distribution and weight jurisdictions according to the maturity of their frameworks rather than assuming universal uncertainty.
