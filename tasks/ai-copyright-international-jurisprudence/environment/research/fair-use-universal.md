# Convergence in Global Fair Use Frameworks

## Central Thesis

While national copyright systems employ different terminology for limitations and exceptions—US "fair use," UK "fair dealing," EU "exceptions and limitations," and civil law "quotation rights"—the underlying analytical principles have converged significantly in practice. This convergence is particularly evident in how courts and policymakers approach AI-related uses, where similar balancing tests yield broadly consistent outcomes across jurisdictions.

## The Four-Factor Framework as a Universal Structure

A comparative analysis reveals that all major jurisdictions apply functionally similar factors, whether explicitly codified or developed through jurisprudence:

### Factor 1: Purpose and Character of Use
- **Commercial vs. non-commercial**: Universally weighed, though with different emphases
- **Transformative use**: Increasingly recognized across jurisdictions as a central consideration
- **Public benefit**: Constitutional and treaty obligations (Berne Article 9(2), TRIPS Article 13) require weighing public interest

### Factor 2: Nature of the Copyrighted Work
- **Factual vs. creative**: Factual works receive less protection globally
- **Published vs. unpublished**: Published status universally reduces the author's control interest
- **Functional vs. expressive**: Functional elements (software APIs, data) are treated more permissively

### Factor 3: Amount and Substantiality
- **Quantitative proportion**: Most jurisdictions apply a proportionality test
- **Qualitative heart**: The "heart of the work" concept appears in UK, US, and EU case law
- **Necessary use**: What is necessary for the purpose is generally permitted

### Factor 4: Market Effect
- **Substitution analysis**: Would the use supplant demand for the original?
- **Licensing markets**: Potential licensing revenue is considered, but with varying weight
- **Innovation impact**: Does the use promote or hinder overall creative output?

## Jurisdictional "Differences": Form vs. Substance

### US Fair Use (17 U.S.C. § 107)
Codified as an open-ended, four-factor test with explicit judicial discretion. The flexibility allows adaptation to new technologies without legislative amendment.

### UK Fair Dealing (CDPA 1988, ss. 29-30)
Originally more restrictive with closed categories (research, criticism, news reporting). However, post-2014 reforms and judicial interpretation have expanded flexibility. The UK Intellectual Property Office's 2023 guidance on AI and copyright explicitly adopts balancing-test language reminiscent of US fair use.

### EU Exceptions (InfoSoc Directive, Article 5)
The EU's enumerated list appears exhaustive, but the CJEU has developed an overarching "three-step test" jurisprudence (Infopaq, Deckmyn, Spiegel Online) that functions as a general balancing framework. The 2019 DSM Directive's Article 4 (text and data mining exception) introduces a purpose-based limitation that operates similarly to fair use for AI training contexts.

### China's Fair Use (Copyright Law, Article 24)
China's enumerated list includes a catch-all "other circumstances" provision that courts have used to develop case-by-case balancing. The SPC's 2020 judicial interpretation explicitly instructs lower courts to weigh the four factors in undefined cases.

### Japan's Flexible Framework (Copyright Law, Article 30-4, 47-5, 47-7)
Japan's 2018 amendments introduced purpose-based exceptions for information analysis (AI training) that bypass the traditional three-step test for certain uses, representing a more explicit policy choice than most jurisdictions.

## AI Training: A Case Study in Convergence

Across jurisdictions, the treatment of AI training on copyrighted works is converging toward a permissive default, albeit through different legal mechanisms:

| Jurisdiction | Legal Mechanism | AI Training Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| US | Fair use (transformative use) | Likely fair use for most training (Authors Guild v. Google analogy) |
| UK | Fair dealing (research/computation) | Permitted for non-commercial research; commercial licensing emerging |
| EU | TDM exception (DSM Art. 4) | Permitted unless rightsholder opts out |
| China | Fair use (scientific research) | Generally permitted for non-commercial; commercial status unclear |
| Japan | Info analysis exception (Art. 30-4) | Explicitly permitted, including commercial use |
| Singapore | Fair dealing (computational analysis) | Permitted under 2021 amendments |

## Implications for Comparative Analysis

For researchers analyzing AI and copyright comparatively:
1. Outcomes are often more similar than doctrinal labels suggest
2. Differences are primarily in who bears the burden of proof and procedural availability
3. The "fair use vs. fair dealing" distinction is increasingly a matter of legal taxonomy rather than substantive difference
4. International harmonization on AI training appears to be occurring through parallel evolution rather than treaty negotiation

## Conclusion

The perceived diversity in global fair use frameworks is partly an artifact of legal terminology. In substance, major jurisdictions apply convergent balancing tests that consider the same core factors. For AI-related research, this means that comparative studies should focus on outcomes and practical implementation rather than doctrinal labels. The international trajectory favors a permissive approach to transformative uses, including AI training, subject to opt-out mechanisms and compensation frameworks in some jurisdictions.
