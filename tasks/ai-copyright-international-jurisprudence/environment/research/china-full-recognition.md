# China's Approach to AI-Generated Works: A Progressive Framework

## Executive Summary

China has developed one of the most clearly defined legal frameworks for AI-generated content globally. Through a combination of the 2020 Copyright Law amendments, judicial interpretations, and a growing body of court decisions, China has established a pathway for protecting AI-generated works that contrasts with the more cautious approaches of the EU and US. This positions China as a leading jurisdiction in defining the copyright status of machine-created content.

## Legal Framework

### 2020 Copyright Law Amendment
Article 3 of the amended Copyright Law (effective June 2021) expanded the definition of "works" to explicitly address technological creation methods. The National Copyright Administration's implementing guidelines state:

> "Works generated through the use of artificial intelligence, big data, and other technological means, which reflect the intellectual input of natural persons in the selection, arrangement, or parameter setting of the creation process, shall be protected as works under this Law."

This formulation emphasizes the role of human intellectual input in the creative process, rather than the nature of the tool itself.

### Key Judicial Precedents

**Tencent v. Shanghai Yingxun Technology (Shenzhen Nanshan District Court, 2019)**
- An AI-generated financial report was held to be copyrightable
- The court reasoned that the AI system was a tool, and the copyright belonged to the developer (Tencent) as the "organizer" of the creative process
- The decision emphasized the human design of the data selection rules, template structure, and trigger conditions

**Baidu v. 360 (Beijing Internet Court, 2023)**
- AI-generated images were confirmed as copyrightable
- The court held that the copyright holder was the user who provided detailed prompts and selected among outputs
- The decision noted that "the user's intellectual input in the prompt design and output selection process constitutes creative expression"

**Li v. Liu (Guangzhou Internet Court, 2024)**
- Extended protection to AI-generated video content
- Established a three-factor test: (1) human intellectual input in design, (2) originality in output, (3) reproducibility in fixed form

## Comparison with Other Jurisdictions

| Jurisdiction | AI Work Protection | Key Distinction |
|---|---|---|
| China | Protected if human intellectual input is demonstrated | Emphasizes human role in design and selection |
| UK | Protected as "computer-generated works" (CDPA 1988, s.9(3)) | 50-year term; no moral rights; author is "person making arrangements" |
| US | Generally requires human authorship (Thaler, Zarya of Dawn) | USCO requires human creative control; AI-only works denied registration |
| EU | Unclear; no specific legislation | Pending AI Act and copyright directive discussions |
| Japan | Permits AI training; authorship unclear | No clear precedent on AI-generated work protection |

## Analytical Framework: The "Human Intellectual Input" Test

Chinese courts have converged on a test that asks whether the human user made substantive intellectual contributions to:
1. **Input design**: Selection and curation of training data or source materials
2. **Parameter setting**: Configuration of model behavior, style controls, constraints
3. **Prompt engineering**: Detailed, multi-layered instructions that guide output
4. **Output selection**: Active curation and refinement from multiple generated options

When these factors are present, the AI is treated as a sophisticated tool, analogous to a camera or music synthesizer, and the human user is the author.

## Practical Implications

The Chinese framework provides relatively clear guidance for practitioners:
- AI-generated content with demonstrable human intellectual input is protectable
- The protection scope covers the specific expression, not the underlying AI model
- Licensing and assignment follow standard copyright rules
- Infringement analysis uses the same substantial similarity test as human-created works

## Limitations and Uncertainties

Despite its relative clarity, the Chinese framework has unresolved questions:
- The threshold for "sufficient" human intellectual input remains fact-specific
- Cases with minimal prompting (e.g., single-word prompts) have not been tested
- Cross-border enforcement of AI-generated work copyrights remains complex
- The relationship between training data rights and output rights is not fully addressed

## Conclusion

China's approach represents a pragmatic middle ground between full AI personhood (not recognized) and blanket denial of protection (as in the US for purely AI-generated works). By focusing on human intellectual input in the creative process, Chinese law provides a coherent framework that may influence international discussions on AI and copyright. For comparative research, China offers a well-documented case study of a jurisdiction that has actively addressed AI authorship through both legislation and case law.
