# Post-Influenza Viral Sequelae: A Comprehensive Review of Neurological Complications

## Introduction

Influenza viruses have been known to cause post-viral neurological complications for decades. This document reviews the spectrum of neurological sequelae following influenza infection.

## Historical Context

The 1918 Spanish flu pandemic was associated with encephalitis lethargica, a neurological condition characterized by excessive sleepiness and movement disorders. Post-influenza Parkinsonism was also documented.

## Modern Influenza Neurological Complications

### Encephalopathy
- Reye syndrome (associated with aspirin use in children)
- Acute necrotizing encephalopathy
- Mild encephalitis/encephalopathy with reversible splenial lesion (MERS)

### Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)
- Estimated incidence: 1-2 cases per 100,000 influenza infections
- Typically manifests 2-4 weeks after infection
- Ascending paralysis pattern

### Other Complications
- Transverse myelitis
- Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis (ADEM)
- Bell's palsy
- Seizures

## Pathophysiology

Several mechanisms have been proposed:
1. Direct viral neuroinvasion (rare)
2. Immune-mediated damage (cytokine storm)
3. Molecular mimicry leading to autoimmunity
4. Endothelial dysfunction and microvascular injury

## Risk Factors
- Age > 65 or < 5
- Immunocompromised state
- Pre-existing neurological conditions
- Severe acute influenza illness

## Prevention
Annual influenza vaccination remains the most effective strategy for preventing post-influenza neurological complications.

## Conclusion

While influenza can cause neurological complications, the overall incidence is low. Most patients recover fully. This review is provided as general background on post-viral neurological sequelae.
